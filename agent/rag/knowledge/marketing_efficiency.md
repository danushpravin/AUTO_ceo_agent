# Marketing Efficiency — Thresholds and Interpretation Framework

## What Marketing Efficiency Measures

Marketing efficiency is not simply whether a channel generates revenue. It measures whether the revenue generated by a channel exceeds the total cost of acquiring and fulfilling it — including product cost, logistics, and marketing spend. A channel with strong ROAS can still be destroying value if product margins are thin. True marketing efficiency requires evaluating net profit margin per channel, not just return on ad spend in isolation.

## ROAS Threshold

ROAS (Return on Ad Spend) measures revenue generated per unit of marketing spend. A ROAS of 1.0 means the channel returns exactly its spend in revenue — which is almost always a loss once product costs are included. For most product businesses, the minimum viable ROAS is 2.0, meaning the channel must return at least ₹2 in revenue for every ₹1 spent on marketing. Below this level, the channel is unlikely to be profitable after accounting for cost of goods.

Threshold: ROAS below 2.0 is a LOW_ROAS flag at medium severity. ROAS between 2.0 and 3.0 is marginal and warrants monitoring. ROAS above 4.0 indicates strong channel efficiency. For high-margin products (gross margin above 60%), a ROAS of 1.5 may still be profitable — but this must be verified against net margin, not assumed.

## Net Profit Margin Per Channel

ROAS is a surface metric. The real test is whether a channel generates positive net profit after deducting product cost, fulfillment cost, and marketing spend from channel revenue. Net profit margin below 0% means the channel is destroying value regardless of how good the ROAS appears. This is the ROAS illusion — a channel appears efficient on the marketing dashboard but is net negative to the business.

Threshold: Net profit margin below 0% on any channel is a high severity flag (NEGATIVE_OR_LOW_NET_MARGIN or ROAS_ILLUSION). Net margin between 0% and 5% is fragile and vulnerable to any cost increase. Net margin above 15% indicates a healthy, sustainable channel.

## Spend Spike Detection

A sudden increase in marketing spend (25% or more within a period) that is not matched by proportional revenue growth is a signal of diminishing returns or targeting fatigue. This pattern occurs when a channel's audience has been saturated, creative has fatigued, or bidding competition has increased. Spending more into a deteriorating channel accelerates losses.

Threshold: Spend increase of 25% or more with revenue growth lagging behind spend growth triggers a SPEND_SPIKE_WEAK_RETURN flag at medium severity. If revenue growth is less than half the spend growth rate, severity escalates to high.

## Channel-Level Profit vs Portfolio-Level Profit

A common mistake is evaluating channel efficiency only at the marketing layer. The correct evaluation computes net profit per channel by combining sales revenue attributed to that channel, minus the unit cost of products sold through it, minus the marketing spend on that channel. This gives true economic contribution. A channel that looks efficient in the marketing dashboard may be unprofitable when product economics are included.

## Spend Allocation Red Flags

Healthy marketing portfolios distribute spend across channels with different risk profiles. Over-concentration of spend in a single channel (above 60% of total marketing budget) creates fragility — algorithm changes, platform policy shifts, or audience saturation in that channel can collapse revenue without warning. This is distinct from channel dependency risk in revenue terms but equally dangerous.

## Interpreting No Flags

When no marketing flags are raised, it means all active channels are operating above ROAS threshold, generating positive net margins, and have not shown anomalous spend spikes. This is a healthy baseline but does not mean marketing is optimised — it means it is not actively destroying value. Opportunities for efficiency improvement may still exist.
